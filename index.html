Where Australia’s Tourists Concentrate

From city crowding pressure to smarter regional distribution

Why does tourism pressure feel especially visible in Sydney and Melbourne during peak travel seasons? This visualisation follows the evidence from seasonal transport pressure, to gateway-state concentration, to a more useful question: where could international visitors be spread without reducing the value of tourism?

1. Pressure 2. Concentration 3. Value 4. Recommendation

1 · Pressure

The crowding story starts with timing

Tourism pressure becomes most visible when visitor movement overlaps with everyday local movement. The first chart therefore compares international travel activity with public transport use in New South Wales and Victoria on the same indexed scale.

01

Do tourism peaks overlap with local transport pressure?

Tourism demand does not replace local demand; it is added on top of it. Indexing each series makes different units comparable without pretending they measure the same thing.

The problem is not simply “too many tourists”. The issue is peak visitor activity overlapping with everyday city movement.

Note: Values are indexed because international arrivals and public transport trips are measured on different scales. For each series, the highest month in 2025 equals 100. This chart shows overlap in timing, not proof of causation.

2 · Concentration

The pressure is uneven because visitors cluster in a few gateways

The next group of charts moves from a national map to regional hotspots. The story shifts from “Australia has many visitors” to “many visitors enter the same states and recognise the same places.”

02

Are international visitors spread evenly across Australia?

The state map shows broad spatial concentration. New South Wales, Victoria and Queensland function as major gateways, which helps explain why crowding is more visible in some places than others.

Tourism pressure is spatially concentrated: a few gateway states receive a much larger share of international trips.

Note: Visitor counts are aggregated from tourism-region international visitor data. The map shows broad state concentration, not exact local crowding within each city.

03

Where exactly are the hotspots?

The bubble map zooms from states into tourism regions. The largest circles sit around gateway cities and iconic coastal destinations, showing that visitor attention is concentrated in recognisable, easy-to-plan places.

Hotspots are not only states. They are specific regions that visitors can easily recognise, reach and include in a first itinerary.

Note: Bubble locations are approximate tourism-region reference points. Use the state filter to focus on one state without changing the story order.

04

Which gateway states dominate visitor arrivals?

This section combines a gateway map, a dynamic share pie and an airline traffic trend so the story moves from location, to time, to concentration in one flow. Readers can compare where visitor pressure is strongest, how the broader travel trend has shifted, and how dominant the three gateway states remain.

Visitor pressure is highly concentrated: New South Wales, Queensland and Victoria together account for 83.6% of international visitor trips.

Note: State totals are based on aggregated international visitor trips from the region-level tourism data used in this story. The line chart uses monthly international airline passenger volumes from BITRE, and the filter keeps the same visual style as the rest of the document.

05

Why do visitors follow the same route?

The route map shows where the main tourism hotspots are located, while the ranking chart shows that a small set of iconic cities and coastal destinations sits clearly ahead of the rest.

Visitor choices are shaped by visibility and familiarity: the same small group of iconic cities and coastal destinations dominates international tourism demand.

What does the route mean?

International visitors do not spread evenly across destinations. Instead, they are drawn toward a familiar chain of iconic cities and coastal hotspots that are easy to recognise, easy to market and easy to include in a first trip.

Note: The map highlights major tourism hotspots and an illustrative route between them. The bar chart ranks the same destinations by international visitor trips to show their relative popularity more precisely.

06

How concentrated is visitor attention?

The waffle chart deliberately uses a different idiom: each square is one percentage point. This makes the concentration story more concrete than another bar chart.

The issue is concentration: many visitors are moving through the same small set of places.

Note: Shares are calculated within the top-20 international tourism regions available in the dataset.

3 · Value

More visitors is not the same as better tourism value

Once concentration is established, the story turns toward solutions. Some places with fewer visitors may still generate high spending or longer stays, which makes them useful candidates for smarter distribution.

07

Do the most visited places create the most value?

Visitor volume alone does not tell the full story. The bubble plot compares popularity with spending per visitor and total expenditure.

This is the turning point: more visitors is not the only goal; better distribution can also create value.

Note: Spending per visitor is calculated from regional expenditure divided by international visitor trips. The square-root x-scale reduces extreme compression from Sydney and Melbourne.

08

Which regions are hidden high-value destinations?

The opportunity map separates regions by visitor volume and spending per visitor. The shaded target zone highlights places that receive fewer visitors but already generate stronger value per visitor.

The strongest alternatives sit in the shaded target zone: lower visitor volume, but above-median visitor value.

Note: Dashed lines show the median visitor volume and median spending per visitor across the top-20 tourism regions. The x-axis uses a square-root scale so smaller destinations remain visible beside Sydney and Melbourne.

09

Which hidden-value regions should be prioritised?

The dumbbell chart compares each region's visitor-volume rank against its spend-per-visitor rank. The gap between the two dots represents the hidden-value score: the wider the bar, the more a region's per-visitor value outperforms its popularity.

The strongest candidates are places where visitor value already looks stronger than popularity.

Note: Hidden-value score is calculated as visitor-volume rank minus spend-per-visitor rank. Higher positive scores indicate stronger priority candidates.

4 · Recommendation

Spread demand toward places that already have capacity

The final step avoids the weak recommendation “send tourists anywhere else”. Instead, it checks whether alternative regions already have some tourism-related business capacity.

10

Where could visitors be spread next?

Capacity is represented with tourism-related businesses. The stacked bars separate employing and non-employing businesses, giving a more informative view than a single total.

Australia does not need fewer tourists. It needs a smarter distribution of tourism demand across regions and seasons.

Note: Business counts are a broad capacity signal only. They do not measure transport capacity, environmental limits or local resident sentiment.

Conclusion

From crowding complaint to distribution strategy

The visual story suggests that tourism pressure becomes most visible when seasonal visitor activity overlaps with local transport use, and when international visitors concentrate in a few gateway states and iconic regions. A better response is not to reduce tourism, but to promote high-value alternatives with enough existing tourism capacity.
